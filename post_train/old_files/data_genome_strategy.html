§ 01 — The Sample Taxonomy

Eight measurable categories

Every sample in the training set will be assigned to one of these categories. Each is defined by a precise combination of measurable signals — not subjective judgement. The categories are not mutually exclusive (a sample can be both rare and hard), so each sample gets a primary label plus secondary tags.

Easy / Prototypical
Learned in the first few epochs. High confidence, never forgotten. Sits deep inside the correct class cluster. Potentially redundant for training.
MCT ρ < 0.10 · high AUM · 0 forgetting events · high prototypicality
Mildly Hard
Requires some learning before being correctly classified. Crosses the decision boundary in the first 20% of training. Not trivial, but the model handles it with moderate feature extraction.
MCT ρ ∈ [0.10, 0.20] · moderate AUM · low forgetting
Hard / Complex
Genuinely complex samples with subtle distinguishing features. The model needs to build deep representations. Crosses the boundary mid-training. Often belongs to visually similar inter-class regions.
MCT ρ ∈ [0.20, 0.60] · low AUM · moderate forgetting · low prototypicality
Very Hard / Near-Boundary
Among the last samples to be correctly classified. Crosses the boundary late (60–80% of training). These samples live right at the decision boundary between classes. Correctly labeled but extremely challenging.
MCT ρ ∈ [0.60, 0.80] · near-zero AUM · high forgetting · high margin variance
Rare / Under-represented
Belongs to a sub-population with very few similar samples. The difficulty is not intrinsic feature complexity, but data scarcity. Often high prediction depth, yet high prototypicality within its own (tiny) cluster.
MCT ρ > 0.50 · low kNN density · high intra-class distance · sharp post-cross rise
Noisy / Mislabeled
Has an incorrect label. The model's margin never stabilises above zero — it oscillates as the model tries and fails to reconcile the contradictory signal. Eventually memorised if training runs long enough, but the pattern is brittle.
MCT ρ = ∞ · negative AUM · high forgetting · high confidence in wrong class · EL2N outlier
Redundant
Highly similar to many other easy samples. Removing it would not affect model performance. Extremely high kNN density in embedding space, very early crossover, and near-zero gradient norm throughout training.
MCT ρ < 0.05 · max kNN density · near-zero grad norm after epoch 2 · high AUM
Boundary / Ambiguous
Legitimately ambiguous — could belong to multiple classes. High confidence disagreement between ensemble members. Margin oscillates around zero even late in training but does eventually settle (unlike noisy). Correctly labeled but inherently uncertain.
MCT oscillating · high margin variance · high ensemble disagreement · moderate AUM

Key distinction — Hard vs Rare vs Noisy: These three categories all show late or absent margin crossover. The experiment is designed to disambiguate them using secondary signals: embedding density separates rare from hard, margin oscillation pattern separates noisy from both, and gradient variance separates boundary/ambiguous from all three.

§ 02 — Metrics & Definitions

The measurement instrument panel

Each sample will have a vector of these metrics computed across training. Together, they form the feature space in which we perform the final categorisation.

Metric Type Definition When Computed Primary Signal For
Margin(t) per-epoch logity(x) − maxc≠y logitc(x) at epoch t. The raw signal from which most other metrics derive. Every epoch All categories
AUM scalar Mean of margin(t) across all epochs. Positive = generally correct, negative = generally wrong. End of training Easy vs Noisy
MCT τ* / ρ scalar First epoch where margin stays positive for k consecutive epochs. ρ = τ*/T normalised. End of training Difficulty gradient
Forgetting Events count Number of times a sample transitions from correctly classified to misclassified across consecutive epochs. End of training Boundary, Noisy
Confidence(t) per-epoch softmax probability assigned to the true class at epoch t. Every epoch All categories
Confidence Variability scalar Standard deviation of confidence(t) across epochs. High variability = unstable learning. End of training Boundary, Noisy
EL2N scalar L2 norm of the error vector (predicted − one-hot target) at a given epoch. Measures how "wrong" the prediction is. Early epochs (1–5) Easy vs Hard
GraNd scalar Expected gradient norm of the loss w.r.t. model parameters for this sample. High = informative to training. Early epochs (1–5) Informative vs Redundant
Per-sample Loss(t) per-epoch Cross-entropy loss for this sample at epoch t. Trajectory shape reveals learning dynamics. Every epoch All categories
Margin Variance scalar Variance of margin(t) across epochs. High = oscillation. Distinguishes noisy/boundary from consistently hard. End of training Noisy vs Boundary
kNN Embedding Density scalar Average distance to k-nearest-neighbours in final hidden layer embedding space. Low density = isolated/rare. Post-training Rare, Redundant
Prototypicality scalar Cosine similarity between a sample's embedding and the mean embedding of its class. High = prototypical. Post-training Easy, Rare
Prediction Depth scalar Earliest layer at which a linear probe can correctly classify the sample. Shallow = easy features, deep = complex. Post-training Feature complexity
Ensemble Disagreement scalar Proportion of independent model runs that disagree on the predicted class. High = inherently ambiguous. Post-training (multi-seed) Boundary, Noisy
§ 03 — Raw Signals to Capture

What your training loop must emit

These are the raw values your modified training loop needs to record. Everything else (AUM, MCT, forgetting events, etc.) is derived from these in post-processing. The goal is to keep the training-time overhead minimal while capturing everything needed.

Per Sample × Per Epoch (during training)
  • Full logit vector — all class logits for this sample at this epoch. From this you derive margin, confidence, EL2N, prediction, and more.
  • Loss value — the per-sample cross-entropy (or whatever your loss function is).
  • Correct/incorrect flag — argmax(logits) == true_label. Cheapest possible signal, enables forgetting events.
  • Gradient norm (optional, epochs 1–5 only) — L2 norm of the gradient of the loss w.r.t. the final layer parameters. Used for GraNd. Expensive, so only record for the first few epochs.
Per Sample × Once (post-training)
  • Final-layer embedding — the representation vector from the penultimate layer of the trained model. Used for kNN density, prototypicality, and UMAP visualisation.
  • Intermediate-layer embeddings (optional) — representations from layers at 25%, 50%, 75% depth. Used for prediction depth probing.
  • True label and sample index — to join everything together.

Storage budget: For a dataset of N samples, C classes, and T epochs — the logit tensor is N × T × C floats. For CIFAR-10 (50k × 200 × 10) this is ~400MB in float32, very manageable. For ImageNet (1.2M × 90 × 1000) this is ~430GB — use float16 or only log every k-th epoch.

§ 04 — Pre-Training Setup

Before training begins

The preparation phase ensures your training pipeline is properly instrumented and your storage is allocated. Nothing in this phase changes your model architecture or training procedure — you're just adding observation hooks.

STEP 01
Data Registry

Create a persistent sample registry — a mapping from each training sample to a unique integer index that remains stable across epochs. This is your join key for all subsequent analysis.

Record: sample_idx, true_label, file_path / row_id (for traceability), class name, and any existing metadata (source, split origin, annotation confidence if available).

Critical: Your dataloader must provide deterministic sample ordering OR emit the sample index alongside each batch. Use a Sampler that tracks indices, not random shuffling without index tracking.

STEP 02
Storage Allocation

Pre-allocate the logit tensor: shape [N_samples, T_epochs, C_classes]. For large datasets, use memory-mapped files (numpy memmap or HDF5) so you don't need to hold the full tensor in RAM.

Pre-allocate the loss tensor: shape [N_samples, T_epochs].

Pre-allocate the correctness matrix: shape [N_samples, T_epochs] — boolean/uint8.

Optionally, a gradient norm array: shape [N_samples, K_early_epochs] where K ≈ 5.

STEP 03
Baseline Run Config

Decide on the training configuration for the cartography run. This should be your standard training setup — same architecture, optimizer, LR schedule, augmentation. The point is to observe normal training dynamics, not to optimise for map quality.

Important choices:

  • Number of epochs T — must be long enough for the model to converge. For CIFAR: 200 epochs. For ImageNet: 90. For custom data: run until validation accuracy plateaus.
  • Logging frequency — ideally every epoch. If T is very large, every 2–5 epochs. But be aware this reduces MCT resolution.
  • Number of seeds — run at minimum 3 seeds (ideally 5) to compute ensemble disagreement. This is essential for distinguishing noisy from boundary samples.
  • Stability window k for MCT — default k=5 epochs. If T is small (<50), use k=3.
STEP 04
Hook Installation

Instrument your training loop with callbacks/hooks. You need two types:

① End-of-batch hook — during the forward pass, before the loss is reduced, capture the per-sample logits and per-sample loss. This requires your loss function to be in reduction='none' mode so you get individual sample losses.

② End-of-epoch hook — after all batches, flush the epoch's data to disk. Also run a full evaluation pass over the training set with model.eval() and torch.no_grad() to get clean logits without dropout/augmentation noise.

Eval pass vs training pass logits: You can record logits from either the training forward pass or a separate eval pass. The eval pass is cleaner (no augmentation randomness, no dropout) and gives more stable margin trajectories. The training pass is cheaper. Recommendation: use the eval pass for the official logits, and skip recording training-pass logits unless you want to study augmentation effects.

§ 05 — During Training

Epoch-by-epoch recording

This is the core data collection phase. Your training loop runs exactly as before, but at each epoch you record the per-sample signals. Here is the exact protocol.

Per-Epoch Recording Protocol
01
Train normally for one epoch. Standard forward pass, loss computation, backward pass, optimizer step. No changes to the training procedure itself.
02
Run an evaluation pass over the full training set. Set model to eval mode. Iterate over the training set with a non-shuffled dataloader (preserving sample order). For each batch, record:
— Logits: the raw output of the model before softmax, shape [batch, C]. Store at logit_tensor[sample_indices, epoch, :].
— Per-sample loss: compute cross-entropy with reduction='none'. Store at loss_tensor[sample_indices, epoch].
— Correctness flag: argmax(logits) == true_label. Store at correct_matrix[sample_indices, epoch].
03
Gradient norms (epochs 1–5 only). For the first K epochs, additionally compute the per-sample gradient norm. This requires a per-sample gradient computation — either via torch.vmap (efficient) or by computing the gradient for each sample individually (slow but simple). Only store the L2 norm of the gradient w.r.t. the last linear layer parameters, not the full gradient. Store at grad_norm[sample_indices, epoch].
04
Flush to disk. After each epoch's eval pass, write the epoch slice to disk (memmap auto-flushes, HDF5 needs explicit flush). This protects against crashes losing all data.
05
Log global stats. Record epoch-level aggregates for sanity checking: mean loss, accuracy, mean margin, fraction of samples with positive margin. This helps verify the recording is working and lets you spot anomalies early.
Overhead Estimate

The extra eval pass per epoch is the main cost. On a single GPU, this typically adds 30–50% wall-clock time to each epoch (since it's a forward-only pass, it's cheaper than training). For a 200-epoch CIFAR run that normally takes 2 hours, expect ~3 hours total.

Gradient norm computation (epochs 1–5) adds ~2–5× overhead for those specific epochs only, so total impact is minimal.

Memory Considerations

The logit tensor doesn't need to live in GPU memory — transfer to CPU after each batch and write to the memory-mapped file. The bottleneck is disk I/O, not GPU memory.

For very large datasets, use float16 for stored logits (saves 50% disk). The precision loss is negligible for margin/AUM computation.

If storage is tight, log every 2nd or 5th epoch instead. This halves MCT precision but is often sufficient.

Live Dashboard — Monitor While Training

Track these aggregates in real-time (TensorBoard / W&B) to verify recording quality and get early signals:

  • Fraction of samples with margin > 0 — should increase monotonically (approximately). If it plateaus early, training may be underfitting.
  • Mean margin per class — shows if some classes are learned much faster than others. Class imbalance shows up here.
  • Number of forgetting events this epoch — should spike during learning rate warmup and decrease over time. A late spike suggests instability.
  • Histogram of per-sample loss — should gradually shift left (toward zero). A persistent right tail = hard/noisy samples.
  • Count of samples that have never been correct — should decrease each epoch. Samples still never-correct at epoch T are your strongest noisy candidates.

Multi-seed protocol: Repeat the entire training run 3–5 times with different random seeds (different weight initialisation and data order, same hyperparameters). For each seed, record the full logit tensor independently. The ensemble disagreement metric is computed post-hoc by comparing the final predictions across seeds. Samples that are always correctly classified across all seeds are genuinely easy. Samples where seeds disagree are inherently ambiguous or boundary cases.

§ 06 — Post-Training Analysis

Building the map

After training completes (all seeds), you have the raw tensors. Now we derive all metrics and classify every sample. This is a pure compute / analysis step — no GPU training needed, just numpy/scipy work.

PHASE A
Derive Scalars

From the raw logit tensor [N, T, C] and correct matrix [N, T], compute the following for each sample:

// For each sample i: margin[i, t] = logits[i, t, y_i] − max(logits[i, t, c≠y_i]) AUM[i] = mean(margin[i, :]) confidence[i, t] = softmax(logits[i, t])[y_i] conf_var[i] = std(confidence[i, :]) margin_var[i] = std(margin[i, :]) EL2N[i] = mean( L2(softmax(logits[i, 1:5]) − onehot(y_i)) ) forgetting[i] = count( correct[i, t]=1 → correct[i, t+1]=0 )
PHASE B
Compute MCT

For each sample, find the first stable crossover epoch τ*:

τ*[i] = min { t : margin[i, t'] > 0 ∀ t' ∈ [t, t+k] } ρ[i] = τ*[i] / T // If no such t exists → ρ = ∞ (or encode as -1) // Default stability window k = 5

Also compute crossover sharpness: the slope of the margin at the crossover point. Steep crossover = decisive learning moment. Shallow crossover = gradual, uncertain.

PHASE C
Embeddings

Extract embeddings from the final trained model (or from each seed's model separately):

  • Penultimate layer embedding for every training sample → [N, D] matrix.
  • Compute kNN density: for each sample, find the mean distance to its k=10 nearest neighbours in embedding space. Normalise by the global mean distance.
  • Compute prototypicality: cosine similarity between each sample's embedding and the centroid of its class.
  • (Optional) Compute prediction depth: train linear probes at layers 25%, 50%, 75%, 100% of the network. For each sample, record the earliest layer where the probe is correct.
PHASE D
Ensemble Stats

If you ran multiple seeds, compute:

  • Ensemble disagreement: fraction of seeds where the final prediction ≠ mode prediction. Ranges from 0 (all agree) to ~0.5 (max disagreement).
  • Seed-averaged AUM: mean of AUM across seeds. Reduces noise in the AUM estimate.
  • MCT consistency: standard deviation of ρ across seeds. Low σ = the sample's difficulty is intrinsic, not seed-dependent.
PHASE E
Classification

Apply the decision rules to assign each sample to a primary category. The rules are applied in priority order (first match wins):

①
Noisy / Mislabeled — ρ = ∞ (never crosses) AND negative AUM AND high forgetting events AND high ensemble disagreement. These are your strongest noise candidates.
②
Boundary / Ambiguous — ρ is finite but late (>0.50) AND high margin variance AND high forgetting AND moderate-to-high ensemble disagreement AND positive (but low) AUM.
③
Rare — ρ > 0.50 AND low kNN density (bottom 10% of embedding density) AND high intra-class distance to centroid. Distinguishes from "hard" by the isolation in embedding space.
④
Redundant — ρ < 0.05 AND top 10% kNN density AND near-zero gradient norm after epoch 2 AND high prototypicality. These samples add almost no training signal.
⑤
Easy / Prototypical — ρ < 0.10 AND high AUM AND zero forgetting events AND high prototypicality. Learned immediately and never forgotten, but not redundant (they have some unique information).
⑥
Mildly Hard — ρ ∈ [0.10, 0.20] AND moderate AUM AND low forgetting.
⑦
Hard / Complex — ρ ∈ [0.20, 0.60] AND low AUM AND moderate forgetting AND adequate kNN density (not isolated).
⑧
Very Hard — ρ ∈ [0.60, 0.80] AND near-zero AUM AND high forgetting. Late-learned but correctly labeled.

Threshold tuning: The ρ boundaries (0.10, 0.20, 0.60, 0.80) are defaults calibrated for ~200-epoch runs. For your specific dataset and training length, visualise the ρ distribution first and adjust boundaries to match natural clusters. The kNN density thresholds (top/bottom 10%) should also be adapted based on the density distribution.

§ 09 — Code Integration Points

Where to hook into your training repo

You already have a working training pipeline. Here are the exact integration points — the minimal set of changes needed to instrument it for data cartography.

Integration Point Map
① Dataloader — Index Tracking ▾

Your dataset's __getitem__ must return the sample index alongside the data and label. If using a standard dataset wrapper, subclass it to add index emission:

class IndexedDataset(Dataset): def __init__(self, base_dataset): self.base = base_dataset def __getitem__(self, idx): data, label = self.base[idx] return data, label, idx # ← add index def __len__(self): return len(self.base)

In the training loop, unpack as data, labels, indices = batch.

② Loss Function — Per-Sample Mode ▾

Switch your loss to return per-sample values. The key change is reduction='none':

# Instead of: loss = F.cross_entropy(logits, labels) # Use: per_sample_loss = F.cross_entropy(logits, labels, reduction='none') loss = per_sample_loss.mean() # for backward pass # Store per_sample_loss for recording
③ Eval Pass — End of Each Epoch ▾

Add a full training-set evaluation at the end of each epoch. This is the core recording step:

def record_epoch(model, train_loader_ordered, epoch, storage): model.eval() with torch.no_grad(): for data, labels, indices in train_loader_ordered: logits = model(data) preds = logits.argmax(dim=1) correct = (preds == labels) per_loss = F.cross_entropy(logits, labels, reduction='none') # Write to memory-mapped storage storage.logits[indices, epoch] = logits.cpu().half() storage.loss[indices, epoch] = per_loss.cpu() storage.correct[indices, epoch] = correct.cpu() model.train()

Important: use a non-shuffled, non-augmented dataloader for this eval pass. Create a separate loader with shuffle=False and only deterministic transforms (resize, normalise — no random crop/flip).

④ Embedding Extraction — After Training ▾

After training completes, register a forward hook on the penultimate layer to capture embeddings:

embeddings = [] def hook_fn(module, input, output): embeddings.append(output.detach().cpu()) handle = model.fc_penultimate.register_forward_hook(hook_fn) # Run full eval pass... handle.remove() all_embeddings = torch.cat(embeddings, dim=0)
⑤ Post-Processing — Metric Derivation ▾

A standalone script (no GPU needed) that reads the stored tensors and computes all derived metrics. This is where AUM, MCT, forgetting events, EL2N, prototypicality, kNN density, and the final categorisation all happen.

This script should output sample_metrics.parquet (one row per sample, columns for every metric) and categories.parquet (the final map).

§ 10 — Validation & Sanity Checks

How to verify the map is correct

A data map is only useful if you trust it. Here are the validation experiments to run after generating your map.

Validation Experiments
V1
Remove-and-retrain (easy/redundant). Remove the bottom 30% of samples by AUM (easiest/most redundant). Retrain from scratch. If the map is correct, accuracy should barely change — confirming these samples are truly redundant.
V2
Noisy label injection test. Take a clean dataset, inject 10% random label noise. Run the full cartography pipeline. The injected noisy samples should overwhelmingly land in the "Noisy" category. Measure recall: what fraction of injected noise is correctly identified?
V3
Rare sample synthesis. Artificially reduce the representation of one class to 10% of its original size. The remaining samples of that class should shift toward "Rare" category. Verify this happens.
V4
Curriculum learning validation. Train a new model using curriculum ordering (easy → hard based on your map). If the map correctly ranks difficulty, this should converge faster in early epochs compared to random ordering.
V5
Visual inspection. For image datasets, randomly sample 20 images from each category and visually inspect. Easy samples should look prototypical. Hard samples should look ambiguous. Noisy samples should have obviously wrong labels. Rare samples should look unusual for their class.
V6
Cross-architecture consistency. Run the cartography pipeline with a different architecture (e.g., ResNet → ViT). Samples categorised as "easy" with one architecture should mostly remain "easy" with another. High correlation = the map reflects data properties, not model quirks.
Final Deliverables
  • sample_map.parquet — one row per training sample with all metrics and primary/secondary category labels.
  • Interactive UMAP visualisation — 2D scatter plot colored by category, with hover to show sample details.
  • Per-class composition chart — for each class, the breakdown of how many samples are easy, hard, rare, noisy, etc.
  • Distribution plots — histograms of AUM, MCT ρ, forgetting events, kNN density, with category coloring.
  • Margin trajectory atlas — representative margin trajectories for each category (the "archetype" curves).
Actionable Outcomes
  • Data cleaning — review and relabel/remove samples flagged as "Noisy".
  • Data augmentation — oversample or augment "Rare" samples to reduce under-representation.
  • Curriculum design — order training from easy → hard for faster convergence.
  • Dataset pruning — remove "Redundant" samples for faster training without accuracy loss.
  • Hard example mining — focus annotation budget on "Boundary" samples where more labeled examples would help most.
  • Model debugging — inspect "Very Hard" samples to understand what your model struggles with.
