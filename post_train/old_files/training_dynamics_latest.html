Data Genome — Neural Training Dynamics

The Data Genome

A complete atlas of metrics that characterise every individual training example — how samples are learned, forgotten, and what their behaviour reveals about the hidden structure of your data.

The Data Genome Project

A training sample is not just a data point.
It has a biography.

Every time we train a neural network, we observe something far richer than a final accuracy number. Across hundreds of epochs, each sample traces a trajectory — crossing decision boundaries, oscillating, stabilising, or never resolving at all. That trajectory is not noise. It is a precise, repeatable fingerprint of what that sample is in the eyes of the model.

The Data Genome is the project of reading those fingerprints systematically — assigning every training example a rich characterisation drawn from the best metrics the training-dynamics literature has produced. The result is not an aggregate statistic. It is a complete, sample-level map of the dataset: who was learned easily, who was forced, who was never learned, and who the model genuinely cannot decide about.

Stronger Models

Knowing which samples genuinely drive learning — and which merely add noise or contradiction — lets us train on data that is structurally efficient. Clean coresets. Better curricula. No gradient budget wasted on examples the model cannot reconcile.

Coreset & Curriculum Selection

The Data Genome directly answers: which 20% of samples carries 80% of the learning signal? Which boundary-proximal examples must be preserved for generalisation? Which rare sub-populations need upweighting? These are decisions that previously required intuition. Here they have metrics.

Label Noise & Data Quality

Mislabeled samples leave a specific and consistent signature in training dynamics — one that is distinct from hard-but-correct examples. The Data Genome surfaces them without human re-annotation, giving a prioritised review queue that concentrates effort where label quality is genuinely uncertain.

Investigations & Analysis

When a model underperforms on a segment, the Data Genome makes the diagnosis tractable: are those samples ambiguous, rare, mislabeled, or simply absent from the training distribution? Each answer implies a different fix — and the fix can be validated by re-running the genome after the intervention.

Fraud Detection

The Premise

In fraud detection, a training sample is not always a transaction. It can be a merchant, a card, an account, a device — any entity in the transaction lifecycle that carries features and a label. A merchant has a risk category and a fraud label. A card has behavioural features and a compromise label. A device has fingerprint features and an abuse label.

What all of these entities share is this: when you train a model, the model's response to each one is a trajectory. That trajectory — how confidently the model learned the entity, how consistently, how late — is not noise. It is a precise fingerprint of what that entity structurally is from the model's perspective.

The Data Genome reads these fingerprints uniformly across every entity type. The result is a complete map: who was easy, who was genuinely hard, who the model cannot decide about, who was never seen enough to learn, and who is almost certainly mislabeled.

Training Dynamics

What a neural network reveals
as it learns

When a model trains, it does not simply accumulate knowledge uniformly. It moves through distinct phases — picking up easy patterns first, then harder ones, and never fully resolving some. Each sample's experience of this process is unique, repeatable, and diagnostic. That per-sample story is what training dynamics lets us read.

Order of Learning

A model does not learn all samples at the same time. Easy, prototypical patterns are acquired first — in the earliest epochs. Hard, boundary-proximal examples come later, if at all. This ordering is not random. It maps directly to sample complexity.

Stability of Confidence

Once learned, does the model stay confident, or does it keep oscillating? High stability means the sample is well-supported by the training distribution. Persistent oscillation means the sample lives in a contested region of feature space — genuinely ambiguous to the model.

Signal vs. Memorisation

A model that learns a sample early and cleanly has found genuine signal. One that only fits a sample very late — or through repeated re-exposure — is memorising. These two paths look identical at the end of training. Only the trajectory tells them apart.

Label Disagreement

When a sample's margin trends persistently negative, the model is not confused — it is consistently confident in the opposite class. The rest of the training data is voting against the label. This is a reliable, architecture-agnostic signature of a mislabeled example.

The metrics in this document are each a different lens on these dynamics. Some summarise the full trajectory into a single number (AUM). Some isolate the moment of commitment (MCT). Some count instability events (Forgetting). Some measure gradient behaviour (EL2N, GraNd). Together, they form a complete characterisation of every sample — a data genome that no single metric can produce alone.

Trajectory-Based Metrics
Computed from the model's per-epoch predictions across the full training run. Capture how and when examples are learned, with what consistency.

01 Area Under the Margin AUM ▾
Intuition

At every training step the model outputs a logit for each class. The margin is the true-class logit minus the highest competing logit. AUM accumulates this gap across all epochs.

A consistently positive margin → easy/clean. A margin that dips negative and stays there → model keeps preferring the wrong class → likely mislabeled.

↑ High AUM → clean / easy ↓ Neg AUM → mislabeled
Formula
AUM(x) = 1/T ∑t=1T
  [ logit(ŷy(t)) − maxc≠y logit(ŷc(t)) ]

// y = true label
// T = total epochs tracked
// Negative AUM → likely mislabeled

02 Margin Crossover Taxonomy MCT ▾
Intuition

The margin starts negative for almost every sample (the model initially can't classify anything). The epoch at which the margin first crosses zero and stays positive tells us a great deal about sample complexity:

Early crossover (0–20% of epochs) → the model grasps this example quickly. Mildly complex but prototypical. Mid crossover (20–60%) → example requires substantial learning. Genuinely complex sub-population. Late / never crossover (>80% or never) → model never confidently memorises → highly likely to be a noisy or contradictory label.

Cross early → mildly complex Cross mid → heavily complex Never cross → noisy label Sparse positive → ambiguous
Definition
margin(t)(x) = logit(ŷy(t)) − maxc≠y logit(ŷc(t))

τ*(x) = min{ t : margin(t') > 0 ∀ t' ≥ t }

// τ* ≤ 0.20·T → mild complexity
// τ* ∈ (0.20,0.60)·T → heavy complexity
// τ* > 0.80·T or ∄ → noisy label
Margin crossover — four sample archetypes over 50 epochs
Mild: crosses <20% epochs — prototypical
Heavy: crosses 20–60% — genuinely complex
Rare/late: crosses >80% — underrepresented
Noisy: never stabilises — likely mislabeled
03 Forgetting Events FE ▾
Intuition

A forgetting event is when a correctly-classified example at epoch t becomes incorrect at epoch t+1. The total count over training signals boundary instability.

Examples with zero forgettings (unforgettable) are redundant and safely prunable. Frequently-forgotten examples straddle the decision boundary and carry high informational value.

0 events → prune safely Many events → keep
Formula
acc(t)(x) = 𝟙[argmax p̂(t)(x) = y]

FE(x) = ∑t=1T-1 𝟙[acc(t+1) < acc(t)]

// counts correct→incorrect transitions
Correctness timeline across 40 epochs
04 First-Split Learning Time FSLT ▾
Intuition

FSLT records the earliest epoch after which a sample is classified correctly for all remaining epochs. Unlike forgetting events (which count every flip), FSLT identifies when the model has permanently committed to the right answer.

FSLT correlates strongly with sub-group frequency — rare examples are learned late (high FSLT), and complex examples are also learned late, making the two difficult to separate using FSLT alone.

Low FSLT → typical / easy High FSLT → rare or complex ∄ FSLT → mislabeled
Formula (Maini et al. 2022)
FSLT(xi, yi) = argmint*
  ( ŷt = yi ∀ t ≥ t* )

// smallest t after which correct always
// = ∞ if never stably correct (mislabeled)

Note: FSLT does not distinguish rare vs complex examples — both have high values. That's where SSFT comes in (see next section).

Split-Based Metrics
Train on partition A, then fine-tune on partition B. Measure which partition-A examples are forgotten — exposing a complementary axis of hardness invisible to first-split metrics alone. Maini, Garg, Lipton, Kolter (NeurIPS 2022)
Requires 2-phase training
05 Second-Split Forgetting Time SSFT ▾
Intuition

After training to convergence on split A, the model is continued on a held-out split B. SSFT records how quickly split-A examples are forgotten during this second phase.

Mislabeled examples are forgotten almost immediately (they were memorised with a brittle pattern — as soon as the model sees real data it overwrites). Complex examples are never forgotten (the model learned a real feature). Rare examples are forgotten slowly as split-B data pulls the model away.

This separates rare vs mislabeled — something FSLT cannot do.

Low SSFT → mislabeled Mid SSFT → rare examples ∄ SSFT (never forgotten) → complex / typical
Formula (Maini et al. 2022)
Phase 1: Train fA on SA to convergence

Phase 2: Fine-tune fA→B on SB

SSFT(xi) = argmint*
  ( ŷtA→B ≠ yi ∀ t ≥ t* )

// smallest t after which WRONG always
// low SSFT → forgotten fast → mislabeled
// ∄ SSFT → never forgotten → complex

Gradient-Based Metrics
Derived from the gradient of the loss with respect to model parameters. Measure how much each sample pushes the model — either in magnitude (GraNd, EL2N) or directional consistency (GradVar).
Requires backward pass
07 Error L2 Norm EL2N ▾
Intuition

Measures the Euclidean distance between the model's predicted probability vector and the one-hot true label at an early checkpoint (typically epoch 5–20).

Large EL2N → model is far from correct prediction → hard, keep. Small EL2N → model already approximately correct → redundant, prune.

Large → hard / keep Small → redundant / prune
Formula
EL2N(x, y) = 𝔼init ‖ p̂(x) − ey ‖2

// p̂(x) = softmax output
// e_y = one-hot label vector
// 𝔼_init = avg over multiple inits
Error vector magnitude — 3 examples in a 3-class problem
08 Gradient Norm GraNd ▾
Intuition

The L2 norm of the per-sample gradient at initialisation. Approximates how far each sample would push the model parameters if trained on alone.

Large GraNd → high-influence sample. Used for coreset selection: a small subset with high aggregate GraNd approximates full-dataset training.

Large → high influence / keep Small → redundant / prune
Formula
GraNd(x, y) = 𝔼θ₀ ‖ ∇θ L(x,y; θ₀) ‖2

// θ₀ = initialisation weights
// 𝔼_θ₀ = avg over multiple inits

09 Gradient Variance GradVar ▾
Intuition

Measures how consistently the gradient vector points in the same direction across multiple training runs or checkpoints for a given sample.

High variance → gradient direction is chaotic → sample is in a conflicted region of the loss landscape, possibly contradicted by other training examples. Low variance → reliable, consistent update signal.

High var → noisy / conflicting Low var → clean, consistent
Formula
GradVar(x) = 𝔼θ ‖ g(x;θ) − 𝔼θ[g] ‖22

// g(x;θ) = ∇_θ L(x,y;θ)
// 𝔼_θ over multiple checkpoints

10 Prediction Depth PredDepth ▾
Intuition

Attach a linear probe to each hidden layer. PredDepth is the shallowest layer whose probe correctly classifies the example.

Easy examples are separable by early layers — they have simple, surface-level features. Hard examples require full network depth — their discriminative features are highly non-linear.

Low depth → easy / prototypical High depth → complex / rare
Formula
PD(x, y) = min{ l : argmax Wl hl(x) = y }

// h_l(x) = activations at layer l
// W_l = trained linear probe at l
// y = true label

11 Prototypicality Proto ▾
Intuition

Average cosine similarity between an example's embedding and all same-class embeddings. Measures how central / representative an example is within its cluster.

High-proto examples look like the canonical member of their class. Low-proto are outliers — atypical, potentially mislabeled, or from a rare sub-population.

High → prototypical / representative Low → outlier / mislabeled

12 Instance-Level Regret Regret ▾
Intuition

All prior metrics ask "how hard is this sample to learn?" Regret asks a fundamentally different question: "for a specific individual prediction, how likely is the model wrong in a way we couldn't foresee?"

Because we only ever observe one realisation of label noise, a model can perform well in aggregate while individual predictions are subject to a lottery of mistakes. Regret captures the mismatch between the error the model thinks it is making (based on noisy labels) and the error it is actually making (against the true label).

Crucially, Nagaraj et al. prove that regret is unavoidable under label noise — even a perfectly-calibrated hedging algorithm incurs positive expected regret. The only escape routes are selective classification (abstaining) or relabelling.

High regret → "lottery" prediction Low regret → reliable prediction Unavoidable if noise exists
Formula
e_true(f,xi,Ui) = 𝟙[f(xi) ≠ ỹi ⊕ Ui]
e_pred(f,xi)  = 𝟙[f(xi) ≠ ỹi]

Regret(f,xi,ỹi,Ui) =
  𝟙[ e_pred(f,xi) ≠ e_true(f,xi,Ui) ]

// ỹ_i = observed (noisy) label
// U_i = noise flip: 𝟙[y_i ≠ ỹ_i]
// ⊕ = XOR (flip operator)
// 𝔼[Regret] = Pr(U=1 | X, Ỹ)

13 Plausible Models & Susceptibility Susceptibility ▾
Intuition

Since we cannot observe the true draw of label noise, we instead sample plausible draws from the posterior noise model and retrain a model on each. The susceptibility of a prediction is how often the model's output changes across these plausible retrainings.

An instance with high susceptibility is one where different equally-plausible label configurations lead to different model predictions — a strong indicator that the prediction is unreliable and should be flagged for review or abstention.

This operationalises regret into something measurable: a sensitivity score per prediction that can be computed without knowing the true labels.

High susc. → flag / abstain Low susc. → prediction robust to noise Requires ensemble over noise draws
Formula
// Sample K plausible clean datasets:
û(k) ~ Posterior(U | X, Ỹ), k=1…K
D̂(k) = { (xi, ỹi ⊕ ûi(k)) }

// Train a model on each:
f̂(k) = train(D̂(k))

// Susceptibility = prediction disagreement:
Susc(xi) = 1/K ∑k 𝟙[f̂(k)(xi) ≠ f̂(1)(xi)]

// High Susc → prediction unstable across
// plausible realisations of the true labels

14 Imbalance Ratio Boundary Bias IR-Bias ▾
Intuition

In imbalanced binary classification, the optimal Bayesian decision boundary for accuracy is not neutral — it intrudes into the minority class region by an amount proportional to the imbalance ratio IR = π₀/π₁. The classifier maximises overall accuracy by simply predicting the majority class more aggressively, at the expense of minority recall.

This is not a modelling failure — it is mathematically optimal for accuracy. The problem is that accuracy is the wrong metric when the minority class is what matters (e.g. fraud, rare disease). Metrics like F1 score, G-mean, and AUC have a different optimal boundary that is shifted back towards the class midpoint.

High IR → severe boundary shift Minority recall → 0 as IR → ∞ F1-optimal ≠ Accuracy-optimal
Formal boundary shift (Gaussian case)
// Accuracy-optimal boundary:
S = { x : ωᵀx + β = ln(π₀/π₁) }

// Shift from class midpoint:
δ = σ²/(μ₁−μ₀) · ln(π₀/π₁)

// F1-optimal boundary:
SF1 = { x : P₁(x)/P₀(x) = F1(x)/(2−F1(x)) · π₀/π₁ }

// δ → ∞ as IR → ∞ (minority recall → 0)
// δ ∝ class overlap σ²/(μ₁−μ₀)

15 Label-Noise-based Rebalancing LNR ▾
Intuition

LNR deliberately flips labels of selected majority-class samples near the decision boundary to minority class labels. These near-boundary samples already have feature distributions similar to the minority class — they are the majority samples most likely to be mistaken for minority anyway.

By relabelling them, LNR realigns the effective prior probabilities seen by the classifier, shifting the decision boundary back towards the F1/G-mean-optimal position without generating synthetic samples, without a balanced auxiliary dataset, and without modifying the classifier itself.

Crucially, the injected noise is asymmetric: only majority→minority flips are made (never the reverse), and only for the most boundary-proximal samples — making the noise maximally beneficial and minimally harmful.

Asymmetric: majority→minority only Model-agnostic / plug-in Near-boundary samples are best candidates No synthetic generation needed
LNR Noise Model
// Asymmetric noise matrix T:
T = [ [1−ε₀, ε₀],
    [ 0, 1 ] ]

// ε₀ = flip rate for majority class
// Minority never flipped (row 2 = [0,1])

// Optimal flip rate (binary case):
ε₀* = 1 − (π₁/π₀) · exp(Δ)

// Δ = correction to align with F1/G-mean boundary
// After LNR: effective class ratio → balanced
// Decision boundary shifts to S^F1

The philosophical inversion

Prior view (detection):

Label noise corrupts the training signal. AUM, SSFT, Forgetting Events, MCT, and Regret are all tools for finding and removing noise to expose the clean underlying signal.

LNR view (intervention):

Label noise is a lever for reshaping the decision boundary. In imbalanced settings, the "clean" boundary is already wrong for the metric you care about. Targeted noise corrects the geometry — noise as surgery, not contamination.

Academic Foundation

Research Lineage

The Data Genome framework synthesises eleven landmark papers spanning NeurIPS, ICML, ICLR, EMNLP, and UAI. The first group are the direct algorithmic foundations of each metric. The second group traces the theoretical lineage of the margin concept — from boosting (1997) through spectral generalization bounds (2017) to empirical gap prediction (2019) — establishing why margin-based statistics carry genuine generalization content, not merely training-time signals.

01
EMNLP 2020
Dataset Cartography: Mapping and Diagnosing Datasets with Training Dynamics
Swabha Swayamdipta · Roy Schwartz · Nicholas Lourie · Yizhong Wang · Hannaneh Hajishirzi · Noah A. Smith · Yejin Choi
Allen Institute for AI  ·  University of Washington
Why it matters The paper that started it all. Introduces the canonical confidence–variability scatter map that forms the backbone of the Data Genome visualisation. Every training sample is characterised by two training-time signals: how often the model predicts the correct label (confidence) and how much that probability fluctuates across epochs (variability). Together, these two axes reveal four fundamental data archetypes — easy-to-learn, hard, ambiguous, and mislabelled — allowing practitioners to audit dataset quality without human re-annotation.

ICLR 2019
An Empirical Study of Example Forgetting during Neural Network Training
Mariya Toneva · Alessandro Sordoni · Remi Tachet des Combes · Adam Trischler · Yoshua Bengio · Geoffrey J. Gordon
Carnegie Mellon University  ·  Microsoft Research Montréal  ·  Mila / Université de Montréal
Why it matters Establishes the concept of a forgetting event: the moment a neural network transitions from correctly classifying a sample to incorrectly classifying it. Samples that are never forgotten are safe to prune; samples that are frequently forgotten are at the boundary between what the model can and cannot learn. This provides a directly actionable pruning criterion and reveals which samples are structurally ambiguous to the model's internal representations.

NeurIPS 2021
Deep Learning on a Data Diet: Finding Important Examples Early in Training
Mansheej Paul · Surya Ganguli · Gintare Karolina Dziugaite
Stanford University  ·  Google Brain
Why it matters Demonstrates that large fractions of training data can be pruned at a very early checkpoint — sometimes as early as epoch 1 — without degrading final model performance. The two scoring functions introduced, GraNd (gradient norm) and EL2N (error L2 norm), give fast per-sample importance estimates that correlate strongly with the long-run learning signal. This validates the idea that training dynamics can be measured cheaply and used to build a compact, high-quality dataset subset.

NeurIPS 2021
Detecting Mislabeled Data using the Area Under the Margin Ranking
Geoff Pleiss · Tianyi Zhang · Ethan R. Elenberg · Kilian Q. Weinberger
Cornell University  ·  University of Texas at Austin
Why it matters Introduces the Area Under the Margin (AUM) statistic: the cumulative signed margin between the logit assigned to the true label and the highest competing logit, summed across all training epochs. Mislabeled samples almost always receive a lower AUM than correctly-labeled ones. The method introduces a clever threshold-estimation trick using synthetic "threshold samples" with deliberately false labels — giving practitioners a calibrated, data-driven cutoff for flagging mislabels without manual review of all samples.

Pang Wei Koh · Percy Liang
Stanford University

While computationally expensive at scale, it establishes the formal causal connection between individual training examples and model behaviour, underpinning gradient-based approximations like GraNd and GradVar.

Indian Institute of Technology Delhi  ·  IIT Delhi
Why it matters Moves beyond dataset-level noise characterisation to instance-level regret: the degree to which a model's decision on a specific sample would change if the training data were different. Introduces the notion of "plausible models" — the set of hypotheses consistent with the observed imperfect demonstrations — and defines susceptibility as how much a sample's decision varies across this plausible set. Highly susceptible samples are the ones where label noise causes the most damage, making this framework critical for prioritising re-labelling effort in fraud detection contexts where label quality varies by region and event type.

ICML 2025
Beneficial Label Noise: Correcting Imbalanced Classification via Label-Noise-based Rebalancing
Youngkyu Hong · Eunho Yang
Korea Advanced Institute of Science and Technology (KAIST)  ·  AITRICS
Why it matters Inverts the conventional wisdom that label noise is always harmful. In imbalanced classification — the defining characteristic of fraud detection datasets — selectively injecting majority→minority label flips for boundary-proximal samples corrects the decision boundary shift induced by class imbalance, without generating synthetic data. The method is model-agnostic, requires no auxiliary balanced dataset, and operates entirely at the data curation stage. For a practitioner mapping their training data with the Data Genome, LNR provides a direct, principled intervention: once the Data Genome identifies the boundary region, LNR operates precisely there.

Theoretical Lineage

These three papers establish why the margin carries generalization content — not just as an empirical signal, but as a theoretically grounded quantity. They are the intellectual ancestors of AUM and MCT: without this lineage, per-sample margin tracking would be a heuristic. With it, it is a principled proxy for each sample's contribution to model generalisation.

T1
ICML 1997
Boosting the Margin: A New Explanation for the Effectiveness of Voting Methods
Robert E. Schapire · Yoav Freund · Peter Bartlett · Wee Sun Lee
AT&T Labs Australian National University UNSW Canberra
Why it matters The foundational paper that connected margin distributions to generalisation in ensemble/voting methods. Proves that generalisation error is bounded by the fraction of training examples with small margin, regardless of the number of base hypotheses — explaining why AdaBoost keeps improving test error after training error hits zero. Establishes the conceptual origin of per-sample margin as a proxy for learning quality. AUM and MCT are the direct descendants of this idea applied to individual training steps in a neural network.
Margin Distribution Boosting · AdaBoost Generalisation Bounds Theoretical Foundation
T2
NeurIPS 2017
Spectrally-Normalized Margin Bounds for Neural Networks
Peter L. Bartlett · Dylan J. Foster · Matus Telgarsky
UC Berkeley Cornell University University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign
Why it matters Extends the Schapire et al. margin framework to deep networks. Proves a formal generalisation bound that scales with the network's spectral complexity (product of spectral norms of weight matrices) divided by the margin — no dependence on number of layers or parameters outside log terms. Crucially, the Lipschitz constant of AlexNet grows throughout training while test error plateaus; but Lipschitz / margin stays flat or decreases, showing the margin normalization correctly tracks generalisation. This is the theoretical justification for why tracking per-sample margins — as AUM does — measures something meaningful about the model's certainty on that sample relative to its overall complexity.
Spectral Complexity Deep Network Bounds Rademacher Complexity Theoretical Foundation
T3
ICLR 2019
Predicting the Generalization Gap in Deep Networks with Margin Distributions
Yiding Jiang · Dilip Krishnan · Hossein Mobahi · Samy Bengio
Google Research
Why it matters Bridges theory and practice: uses normalised margin distributions across multiple hidden layers to predict actual generalisation gaps with R² ≈ 0.97 across 750+ trained networks. Three key insights directly inform the Data Genome: (1) margins must be measured at hidden layers, not just the output — each layer reveals different geometry; (2) raw margins must be normalised by the square root of activation variance (total variation) to remove scale artifacts; (3) the full margin distribution (quartile summary) predicts gaps far better than any single statistic. Confirms empirically that a sample with a persistently negative or near-zero margin is genuinely pushing the model towards a worse generalisation region, not merely a hard example.
Margin Distribution Generalisation Gap Prediction Multi-Layer Analysis Theoretical Foundation
